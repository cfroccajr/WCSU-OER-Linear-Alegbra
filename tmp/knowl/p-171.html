Above in InvestigationÂ 3.1.3 any vector of the form \(\vec{v}=\left\lt-z,-z,z\right\gt\) maps to \(\left\lt 0,0\right\gt\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} T\left( \begin{array}{c} -z \\ -z \\ z \end{array} \right)= \left( \begin{array}{c} -z+z \\ -z+z \end{array} \right)= \left( \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right). \end{equation*}

And so we could show that the kernel of \(T\) is \(span\left\{\left\lt-1,-1,1\right\gt\right\}\text{.}\) Significantly, we can use this to rewrite every element of the domain as

\begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{r} x\\ y \\z \end{array} \right) = \left( \begin{array}{r} x+z\\ y+z \\0 \end{array} \right) + \left( \begin{array}{r} -z\\ -z \\z \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}

so that it is the sum of something in the kernel and something that is not in the kernel unless it happens that \(x=y=-z\text{.}\) This is a small example of a much more general truth, and we will use this later when we discuss orthogonal projections and curve fitting.
