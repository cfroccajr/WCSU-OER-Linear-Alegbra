Further if we replace \(\mathcal{B}\) with

\begin{equation*} \mathcal{B'}= \left\{ \left[ \begin{array}{r} \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \\ 0 \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{r} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{r} -\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \\ 0 \\ \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \end{array} \right] \right\} \end{equation*}
we have an orthonormal basis since all the vectors now have length one and are perpendicular. Moreover, since all of these vectors point in the same direction as the vectors of \(\mathcal{B}\) we can call this the normalization of \(\mathcal{B}\text{.}\)
